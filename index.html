—————— Profile ——————

If the name Agust D is a little strange, you might as well reverse the letters - D T Suga, another identity representing SUGA, a member of South Korea's top boy group BTS.

Agust D is the name used by Suga when he was active in Korean rap scenes before joining BTS, and D T is abbreviated from the Hip-Hop group D-Town he participated in.

Influenced and inspired by Epik High since he was a child, whether he is in a global phenomenal pop idol group or not,

he has always maintained the exploration of the essence of rap creation.

In 2016, he paid tribute to his struggle in the first personal mixtape "Agust D" during the epidemic.

The second mixtape "D-2" won the 11th place on the Billboard album chart.

—————— Artwork ——————

AGUSTD's second solo mixtape D-2 (2020), featured a track titled Daechwita. The video is the MV of this song.

Following up on mixtapes Agust D (2016) and D-2 (2020), D-DAYconcludes a self-reflective trilogy with a potent statement of liberation from societalpressures,

past regrets and future fears. With commanding yet intimate songcraft,

hereimagines tradition ("Haegeum") and pairs boom-bap with honeyed R&B and a killer hookfrom IU ("People Pt.2")

among other highlights including a collab with the late,

laudedpolymath Ryuichi Sakamoto ("Snooze").

D-2 is the second mixtape released by South Korean rapper Agust D, better known as Suga of BTS,

following his eponymous debut mixtape released in 2016.

The mixtape was released on May 22, 2020, through Big Hit Entertainment,

along with the lead single "Daechwita" (Korean: 대취타). The mixtape consists of ten tracks, with songs sung and rapped in both Korean and English.

Its lyrics share the rapper's viewpoint of the contemporary world.

The final cut of Agust D comprises ten tracks on SoundCloud and eight tracks on streaming and download services.

Critics viewed Agust D as a major departure from his previous catalog as a member of BTS with a hardcore rap style highlighting his underground influences.

Billboard and Fuse complimented the album's divergence from his contemporaries in K-pop with its vulnerability and Agust D's self-production.
